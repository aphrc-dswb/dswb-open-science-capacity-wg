# Speaker - Reina Camacho Toro


### Speaker bio

Dr. Reina Camacho Toro is an experimental particle physicist working on the ATLAS experiment at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN. She has a strong background in data analysis, mathematical modelling, and statistical analysis, alongside extensive experience in the research and development of silicon particle detectors and online event selection systems.

Beyond her research, Dr. Camacho Toro has spent several years developing scientific and educational capacity-building initiatives aimed at advancing social progress. She is currently a researcher at the Laboratoire de Physique Nucléaire et de Hautes Énergies (LPNHE), a laboratory of the French National Centre for Scientific Research (CNRS), and serves as co-coordinator of the LA-CoNGA Physics project.

   * Why does science diplomacy matter for open science? and vice versa?
       * ﻿﻿Science diplomacy: the use of scientific collaboration to advance diplomatic, development, and knowledge goals across borders (Royal Society \& AAAS, 2010).
       * ﻿﻿Open science: removing barriers to scientific knowledge (UNESCO
       * Recommendation, 2021), including publications, data, software, and processes.
       * ﻿﻿The COVID-19 pandemic made the consequences of not connecting them viscerally clear.
   * Methods and Frameworks:
       * What was done:
           * ﻿﻿Quantitative survey of 50 open science organisations (non-governmental, non-policy organisations) across Latin America, including NGOs, university networks, publishing platforms, research consortia, and libraries.
           * ﻿﻿Two qualitative focus groups with open science experts from across the region
           * ﻿﻿Mixed methods analysis of activities, barriers, impacts, and policy connections
       * Why non-policy actors (university networks, libraries, research consortia, etc?)?
           * ﻿﻿Because of their higher institutional stability in the face of governmental turnover
           * ﻿﻿A challenge very familiar across the Global South
   * Science Diplomacy Framework:
       * Dimension
           * ACCESS
               * What it means: Securing resources and infrastructure through international partnerships
               * Joining CERN; accessing European data repositories
           * PROMOTION
               * Showcasing national/regional work internationally
               * Presenting LATAM diamond OA at UNESCO


           * INFLUENCE
               * Shaping policy and public opinion on open science
               * Contributing to national legislation or UNESCO recommendations
   * Study Findings regarding Open Science Diplomacy:
       * Finding 1: Open science diplomacy was being practised, but it wasn't named that term.
       * Finding 2: 
           * Cultural resistance within institutions: reluctance to change established publishing, evaluation, and data-sharing practices
           * Financial constraints: APC costs for international commercial journals; lack of structural open science funding
           * Lack of recognition as a career metric: 
       * Finding 3: Collaboration drives policy influence:
           * Statistical findings:
               * Organisations hosting collaborative events with foreign institutions were significantly more likely to generate science policies
               * Organisations with government support showed higher policy influence
           * Most cited positive impacts of open science:
               * Equal opportunity in access to scientific information
               * Establishment of new cross-border collaborations
               * Greater reproducibility of scientific work
               * Increased visibility of regional scientific journals
       * Finding 4: Latin America lacks an open science cloud
           * Compared to Africa, the Latin America region doesn't have a contemplated open science cloud.
       * Finding 5: The recommended path forward
           * For organisations:
               * ﻿﻿Advocacy with governments and international bodies
               * ﻿﻿Community-building and sharing of best practices
               * ﻿﻿Training beyond researchers (science managers, policymakers)
               * Name what you do: if your organisation connects across borders, promotes regional work globally, and shapes policy, that is science diplomacy
                   * DiploCientifica
           * For policymakers:
               * ﻿﻿Standardise and protect scientific data (FAIR principles)
               * Invest in sustainable shared infrastructure
                   * The sustainability part of the projects funded is quite important, and if we do not have that, the work done previously can disappear.
                   * It is important that we explore taking into account the various infrastructures.
               * Create recognition and incentive systems for open science
               * ﻿﻿Fund open science initiatives structurally, not only project-by-project, taking into account local realities. We shouldn't automatically import foreign policies or methods, and expect them to work same way for us.

   * Enabling cross-border data collaboration: Trust-Building Frameworks: The Human Infrastructure
       * Trust is the most under-theorised enabler. Legal agreements and technical standards fail without it. Our LATAM focus groups emphasised: building healthy, sustained communities is as important as any policy document.
       * Non-policy actors as trust builders: civil society, universities, and research networks maintain the continuity of relationships across political cycles (particularly when governments are unstable or change frequently)
       * A cross-regional example in practice: A joint initiative between 2i2c, The Carpentries, MetaDocencia, and Open Life Science was funded to provide cloud workflow training for open science communities in both Latin America and Africa (co-designed, not exported from the North)
