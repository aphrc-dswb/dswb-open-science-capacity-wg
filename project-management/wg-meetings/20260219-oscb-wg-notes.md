# Notes

We must undertake a concerted global effort to encourage and invest in the creation of digital public goods: open source software, open data, open AI models, open standards, and open content.

These digital public goods should adhere to privacy and other applicable laws and best practices, do no harm, and help attain the SDGs.

## DPGA Overview

The DPGA is a member-based organisation.

Some countries within Africa that have this alliance are:

- Sierra Leone  
- Malawi  
- South Africa  
- Nigeria  
- Ethiopia  
- Rwanda

## Strategic Objectives and Targets

### Objectives

Digital public goods with high potential for addressing critical development needs and urgent global challenges are discoverable, sustainably managed, and accessible for government institutions and other relevant implementing organisations.

UN institutions, multilateral development banks, and other public and private institutions that are highly relevant for supporting implementation of digital technologies have the knowledge, capacity, and incentives to effectively promote and support adoption of DPGs.

Government institutions have the information, motivation, and capacity to effectively implement DPGs that address country needs, including planning, deploying, maintaining, and evolving their digital public infrastructure.

Countries have public sector capacity and vibrant commercial ecosystems in place to create, maintain, implement, and incubate DPGs locally.

### Targets

**Fighting Information Pollution and Restoring Trust**

By the end of 2028, sustainably maintained and highly relevant digital public goods:

- Have enabled diverse groups of stakeholders to take action and improve collaboration to fight information pollution and enhance information integrity  
- Have enabled new norm-shaping technologies to scale that help restore trust, protect, and advance human rights

### Making Digital Public Goods the Norm for Preventing and Responding to Urgent Global Challenges

By the end of 2028, digital public goods will be part of the international community's default approach for rapidly empowering relevant stakeholders to prevent and respond to urgent global challenges.

This includes:

- A globally recognised and scalable methodology for rapidly establishing communities of practice  
- Identification of relevant digital public goods for preventing and responding to urgent global challenges  
- Establishment of financing instruments to fund digital public goods of high relevance

### DPGA Members in Africa

Sierra Leone, Nigeria, Rwanda, Malawi, South Africa, and Ethiopia together reflect the diversity of Africa's digital starting points.

- **South Africa** represents institutional and infrastructure maturity  
- **Nigeria** embodies entrepreneurial scale and rapid ecosystem growth  
- **Rwanda** showcases coordinated, policy-led digital transformation  
- **Ethiopia** reflects large-market transition potential  
- **Malawi** highlights early-stage digital capacity building

Collectively, they demonstrate that digital public goods advancement in Africa is shaped by different structural realities — from scale and market dynamism to governance strength and inclusion challenges.

## Digital Public Goods (DPGs)

Digital Public Goods are information content that includes:

- open source software  
- open data sets  
- open content collections  
- open AI systems

They must:

- adhere to privacy and other applicable laws and best practices  
- do no harm  
- help attain the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)  
- comply with the DPG Standard

The **DPG Standard** is maintained by the DPGA.

---

### Importance and Benefits of DPGs

- Open-source and accessible, meaning any country can freely adopt and adapt them for their specific needs.
- Free means accessible and possibly less expensive than alternative tools, but not free as in no cost or no capacity requirements.
- Give countries greater control over how they build and enhance their digital public infrastructure.
- Customisable, enabling countries to create more inclusive digital solutions tailored to their local context.
- Often cheaper and faster to implement than proprietary solutions.
- Allow countries to avoid vendor lock-in and high costs associated with proprietary software licenses and updates.

Additional insights:

- The global value of open source is about **9 trillion dollars**.
- Companies would have to pay about **3.5× more** for proprietary software.
- For every **€1 invested in open source**, about **€4–5 in public value** is generated.

---

## DPGs for DPI

Countries can use DPGs to build components of their digital public infrastructure in a safe, inclusive, and interoperable way.

### Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI)

DPI is composed of core components such as:

- payment systems  
- secure data exchange  
- digital ID

These systems enable countries to deliver essential services and create economic opportunity across many sectors.


### DPG Standard Requirements

- Use of approved open licenses  
- Clear ownership  
- Platform independence  
- Documentation  
- Mechanisms for extracting data  
- Adherence to privacy and applicable laws  
- Adherence to standards and best practices  
- Do no harm

Countries involved must renew their alliance annually to ensure consistency in practice.


### DPG Registry

The **DPG Registry** lists digital solutions that:

- have applied for DPG recognition  
- have been proven to meet the DPG Standard

It is a global resource to support:

- governments  
- international organisations  
- other stakeholders

It helps them discover open-source solutions designed to support the attainment of the Sustainable Development Goals.


### Geography

A large part of the DPG solutions are in:

- North America

Some are in:

- Asia

Fewer are in:

- Africa  
- South America  
- Oceania

---

### Some DPGs in Africa

- **Ushahidi:** Originated in the use case of verifying election results, but has evolved into a tool used to fight misinformation and disaster reporting.

- **OpenFn:** Workflow automation platform for integrating critical systems.

- **CHAT: Convexity Humanitarian Aid Transfer Solution**

- **African Storybook:** Open access to picture storybooks in African languages for literacy.

- **KotaniPay:** Blockchain-to-local payment channel connector for stablecoins.

- **Community Health Toolkit:** Open-source digital health platform for community health systems.

- **Magasin:** Cloud-native open-source end-to-end data platform.

### Challenge

- It is hard to map where these solutions end up being deployed since they are open source.
- It is also difficult to know if they are implemented in commercial solutions.

**OpenFn Example**

OpenFn circumvented this challenge by building a tool that enables users to gift them back usage data.

Current statistics:

- **52 active deployments in 29 countries**
- **~40% of active instances** concentrated in just **5 countries**
- **19 developing countries vs 10 developed countries**
- **62% of active instances** in countries running more than one instance
- **65% of active instances** are in developing countries

Distribution is broad and balanced — presence spans both high-income and emerging markets.

## Conceptual Tension

**“As open as necessary, as closed as possible.”**

"Open" is not automatically "just".

The DPG standard is a standard for the **design of information systems**, not a normative framework for how these systems should be **used and governed**.

The global DPG discourse often assumes the “public” is the nation-state, but this raises questions about **community stewardship and governance of digital commons**.

### Relevance to Africa

Digital Public Goods are particularly relevant in Africa — not as an abstract governance ideal, but because the continent's economic, infrastructural, and institutional realities make shared digital infrastructure strategically necessary.

They represent an **economic opportunity** for many African countries.

**Case Study**

- Mojaloop for Rwanda

### Key Realities

Africa varies widely in:

- connectivity  
- institutional capacity  
- governance maturity  
- market depth

Internet access disparities require:

- mobile-first systems  
- offline-aware systems  
- bandwidth-sensitive systems

DPGs can reduce duplication and cost, but **do not automatically create the institutional capacity** needed to procure, integrate, govern, and sustain them.

### Implementation Barriers

- uneven implementation capacity  
- thin vendor markets  
- weak long-term financing  
- procurement bias toward proprietary systems  
- limited technical assurance

**These challenges play out differently across:**

- South Africa  
- Nigeria  
- Rwanda  
- Ethiopia  
- Malawi

## Africa as Contributors

Africa is not just an adopter of DPGs but also a contributor.

DPGs promise:

- cost efficiency  
- interoperability  
- shared standards

These are vital for inclusive digital transformation.

## Open Data and Open Science

Open data and **FAIR principles** support the technical interoperability promise of DPGs.

African open science and **decolonial data governance** broaden what we mean by a public good.

They emphasise balancing openness with:

- consent  
- community authority  
- equitable benefit  
- cultural intelligence

There are many ways to contribute.

African actors are not just implementers — they are redefining how digital public goods should be governed globally.
