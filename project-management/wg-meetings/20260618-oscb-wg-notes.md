# Speaker - Sven Ouzman

### Speaker bio

Dr Sven Ouzman is an archaeologist and heritage specialist whose work explores the intersections of rock art, graffiti, heritage, Indigenous knowledge, intellectual property, cross-cultural contact, and understandings of time. His research focuses on collaborative projects where archaeology, heritage, identity, and indigeneity intersect, particularly in the Global South. These projects often include strong outreach and advocacy components, ranging from measuring the carbon footprint of fieldwork to considering both Indigenous and Western legal frameworks governing heritage. He also contributes to the Scholarship of Teaching and Learning through work on equity, diversity, and cultural safety.



Dr Ouzman completed his BA and Honours degrees at the University of the Witwatersrand, South Africa, before serving as Head of the Rock Art Department at the National Museum in Bloemfontein. He later earned both his MA and PhD from the University of California, Berkeley, and has held academic and curatorial positions at the University of Pretoria, Iziko South African Museum, and The University of Western Australia, where he currently serves as Graduate Research Coordinator and is a Senior Fellow of the Higher Education Academy.

Over the course of his career, Dr Ouzman has led research projects across Australia and southern Africa, documenting more than 4,000 rock art sites, conducting over 20 archaeological excavations, and producing more than 200 site reports. He has authored over 70 academic publications and more than 80 other publications, established six rock art site museums, and curated ten exhibitions.

## Notes

* Topic: CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance and Data Sovereignty
   * "For every document on civilisation that exists, there is another on eon barbarism"
       * Indigenous: Someone who comes from a place.
       * Aboriginal: Having existed in a region since the beginning or earliest known time
   * Intellectual Property when it comes to indigenous usually means as a group, and not individual.
   * Issues
       * Who is (and isn't) indigenous? 
       * Individual versus communal ownership
       * Time limitations
       * Do we have different standards for indigenous and non-indigenous data? 
   * "The inherent right of Indigenous Peoples to govern the collection, ownership, access, and control of data about their communities, tures, and knowledges. It seeks to restore control over data that has historically been exploited and to ensure data reflects Indigenous values, priorities, and self-determination, rather than colonial framework" - Indigenous Data Sovereignty (IDaS), Arts Law Australia
   * Definitional deficiences - "DATA" 
       * Data are NOT neutral 'things' to be 'mined'
       * Data are facts* about things (indeed, data are artefacts)
   *        * assumed as facts relative to a particular problem and sets of data creation techniques
       * Data are what we produce out of recognising, observing, recording, measuring, describing and understanding 'things'
       * In some ontologies data are sentient entities and have reciprocal relationships with people
   * Living "data" and their rights
       * Not just 'data'
       * What about human remains? 
   * Is FAIR das and CAREful?
       * Before data is FAIR, it has to be CAREful
           * FAIR: Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable
           * CARE: Collective Benefit, AUthority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics
       * CARE requires accountability from researchers, before they make data FAIR
       * "The current movement toward open data and open science does not fully engage with Indigenous Peoples rights and interests. Existing principles within the open data movement (e.g. FAIR: findable, accessible, interoperable, reusable) primarily focus on characteristics of data that will facilitate increased data sharing among entities while ignoring power differentials and historical contexts. The emphasis on greater data sharing alone creates a tension for Indigenous Peoples who are also asserting greater control over the application and use of Indigenous data and Indigenous Knowledge for collective benefit.
       * This includes the right to create value from Indigenous data in ways that are grounded in Indigenous worldviews and realise opportunities within the knowledge economy. The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance are people and purpose-oriented, reflecting the crucial role of data in advancing Indigenous innovation and self-determination. These principles complement the existing FAIR principles, encouraging open and other data movements to consider both people and purpose in their advocacy and pursuits."
       * Not everything can be open and made publicly available with regard to FAIR, but FAIR principles and CARE can be used together.
   * Laws and Ethics
       * Laws are not the same in different parts of the world.
       * Ethics is not the same as law. 
       * While there is an argument about which is higher between ethics and laws, defying ethics can sometimes have really great consequences. In organisations, it is strong enough to revoke a license and render you unable to practice for a long time.
   * Data as a Process (and responsibility):
       * Do you know where (all) of 'your' data comes from, including 'legacy' data?
       * Do you know how 'your' data were collected?
       * Has this data been used before, and will it be reused in the future?
       * Have you ever refused to use data because of concerns about how it was obtained/ used?
       * What happens to data after you have used it?
   * Data Costs, Benefits and Iniquities
       * 'Cost' is used both in $$ terms and to indicate the broader value of data to one or more source and/or user communities
       * What does the data we use 'cost' in both of the above senses?
       * What is the data 'worth' to us - and to source communities?
       * Do our research and administrative budgets compensate all partners in the research directly or in-kind?
   * What is culture and cultural data worth? 
       * $150 million Australian dollars was recently awarded in a case of cultural loss
       * $100,000 AUD for economic loss
   * Research considerations:L
       * What is the carbon footprint of your research? 
           * Even for data being received
       * Should all research have a climate change element to offset its carbon footprint? 
   * Decoloniality and a Third Way
       * Current practice has imperial, colonial, and neo-liberal bias; often invisible
       * We need first to recognise bias and then try collaboratively find ways to overcome bias and replace it with a more reflexive, contingent practice
   * Ways forward
       * Ensure FPICCin new projects. "Come through the door, not the window" Andries Steenkamp, 2016.
       * Sign binding research contracts that are co-designed or stakeholder-led
       * Embrace two-way or multi-way learning
       * Help build capacity in partners if it lacks
       * Ensure contract allow parties to say 'NO' and for that to be binding
       * Keep data in trust (not owned) and require permission to use and re-use
       * ﻿﻿Re-assign existing IP and use licenses
       * ﻿﻿Have tiered database access (public-restricted)
       * ﻿﻿Digital repatriation of data(?)
       * Tag data (using CITES model) so any datum can be tracked, uses known and compensations made if required. Ensures ethical data and ethical data use.
       * Ensure budgets compensate all stakeholders for time, expertise, cultural safety and intellectual contribution in an ongoing way. What percentage of your cash and/or in-kind budget benefits stakeholders? [40% is a good general average]
