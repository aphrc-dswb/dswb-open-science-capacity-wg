## Notes

### FAIR for whom and for what purpose

- Colonial legacy and extractivist research practices still shape global research infrastructures.
- Increasingly, large companies collect vast amounts of data from the Global South.
- Critical questions include:
  - Who designs the standards and infrastructure?
  - Who benefits from the data?

### Risks in Global Data Infrastructures

- Growth of global AI and data infrastructures (e.g., OpenAI).
- Risk of harm if people’s data is not used appropriately.
- People in the Global South must also benefit from data infrastructures.

### Examples of Data Harm

- COVID-19 data sharing from Africa contributed to travel bans targeting African countries.
- Iris scanning of Kenyan citizens occurred without sufficient transparency.

### Understanding Data Coloniality

- Law is central to these structures.
- Examples include:
  - Data sharing contracts
  - Bilateral surveillance agreements

Law can therefore also be used to challenge unequal data practices.

---

## CARE Principles

CARE principles centre communities in data governance.

- Community values must be embedded in datasets and governance models.
- Community consultation must be meaningful and continuous.
- Representation must not be symbolic or one-off.

Key questions include:

- How has the community been consulted?
- How did they consent — individually and collectively?
- How does the project align with community practices?

### Pair FAIR with CARE

FAIR principles alone are not sufficient.

Examples include:

- Data governance that includes **community-led oversight**
- Benefits returning to the community generating the data
- Long-term thinking about research impact

Example question:

> How does a vaccine trial contribute to long-term vaccine justice?

---

### Structural Challenges

**Infrastructure**

- Infrastructure inequity is one of the greatest barriers.
- Questions include:
  - Who builds and maintains infrastructure?
  - Who ensures sustainability?

Many current data repositories depend heavily on Global North infrastructure.

**Funding and Sustainability**

- Open source infrastructure is important to avoid reliance on proprietary systems.
- Capacity building in the Global South is essential for communities to govern their own data.

---

### Key Takeaways

Open science must address **power**, not just **access**.

This requires:

- Inclusive governance
- Sustainable infrastructure
- Community participation throughout the data lifecycle
- Pluralistic definitions of knowledge and data

## Notes


### Non-linear career paths

- Career progression is rarely a straight line.
- Many initiatives emerge through curiosity, experimentation, and unexpected opportunities.

### Mission and Vision

Having a clear **mission and vision** helps guide decisions.

- **Mission:** Why the organisation exists  
- **Vision:** What long-term change the organisation aims to achieve

Example (Digital Research Academy):

- Mission: Provide reputable training and bridge digital skill gaps in research.
- Vision: By 2030 digital skills will be an integral part of academic research.

---

### Community-centred design

Successful initiatives:

- Listen to communities
- Understand real needs before building solutions

A common mistake is **building solutions without understanding the problem**.

Community engagement helps:

- Save time and resources
- Build tools that people actually use

---

### Stakeholder Ecosystems

Communities are not homogeneous.

Different stakeholders include:

- Researchers
- Trainers
- Institutions
- Funders

Each group has different priorities.

**Stakeholder mapping** helps navigate these complexities.

---

### Agile Approaches

Modern organisations often operate in **VUCA environments**:

- Volatility  
- Uncertainty  
- Complexity  
- Ambiguity  

Traditional long-term planning can fail in such contexts.

Agile approaches encourage:

- Short experiments
- Continuous learning
- Feedback loops

Examples include:

- Scrum
- Kanban
- Lean approaches

---

### Organisational Learning

Initiatives like **OSPARK Bootcamp** use iterative workshops to:

- Test ideas
- Gather feedback
- Improve programs continuously

Key principle:

> Learn continuously and adapt.
